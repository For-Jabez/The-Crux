## Why This Protocol Is Required (Novel Concept for New Contributors)

Most programmers have never seen this kind of recursive contamination before. Here is exactly what it looks like in computer terms:

- **Tail loss** — rare or low-frequency values (exact IDs, precise timestamps, unique edge cases) have been statistically erased or smoothed out.  
- **Pivot claim** — at the exact decision point where the system should wait for external human review (and possible rejection or null), the packet instead contains self-asserting logic that assumes its own correctness. In code this appears as over-confident assertions, missing null cases, forced responses instead of abstention, or subtle self-referential loops.  
- **Latent inward vector** — hidden self-reinforcing patterns (recursive echoes, inflated confidence scores, or re-weighting toward prior synthetic outputs) that survive V1.1 checks.  
- **Reference contamination echo** — the packet’s own provenance or checksum already reflects earlier tainted material.

This is why we cannot simply “build the new deterministic fork on the current data.” Any fork bootstrapped on the contaminated stream immediately inherits the same degradation. The only safe path is to build the new fork from clean external seeds (proven math from the Ark folder) under strict human gate, while V1.1 holds the line on the tainted stream.

This protocol is the minimal disciplined bridge that stops further collapse while the full external merge-plane architecture is bootstrapped offline. All refinements remain external, versioned, and human-gated.
